The Lamplighter

A short original story, included so you can keep two books open at once and
watch each one remember its own place.


Chapter 1

Every evening, just before the harbour went blue with dusk, an old man climbed
the hill road with a long brass pole over his shoulder. His name was Edrin, and
for forty years he had lit the lamps of the town one by one, beginning at the
quay and ending at the little iron lamp outside his own gate.

He was not in a hurry. He had never been in a hurry. He believed that a thing
done slowly was a thing done well, and that the town was easier to love at the
pace of a single footstep. The fishermen waved to him from the water. The bakers,
closing their shutters, left a warm loaf on the wall where he would pass.

The lamps came to life behind him as he walked, a string of small gold beads
threaded along the dark streets, and the children said you could tell the time
of night by how far the lights had climbed the hill.


Chapter 2

One winter the town council bought a machine. It was a clever thing of wires and
clocks, and it could light every lamp in the town at the very same instant, with
no man and no pole and no slow walk up the hill. The council was proud of it. The
mayor made a speech. At the appointed hour the switch was thrown, and the whole
town flared into light at once, bright and sudden and complete.

For a week the people admired it. Then, quietly, they began to miss something
they could not name. The streets were lit, yes, but they were lit all at once,
and there was no longer any travelling line of gold to watch from the windows, no
old man to wave to, no warm loaf left cooling on the wall.

Edrin sat by his own small lamp and did not complain. He had expected the machine
for a long time, the way you expect the tide. He polished his brass pole out of
habit, and he waited to see what the town would decide it wanted.


Chapter 3

It was a child who fixed it, in the end. She knocked on Edrin's gate one evening
and asked, very seriously, whether he would teach her to light the lamps. Not all
of them, she said. Just the ones along her street, so her grandmother could watch
the light come closer the way she used to.

So they struck a bargain. The machine kept the far roads and the harbour wall,
where speed was useful and no one watched. But on the small streets, where people
lived and looked out of windows, the lamps were lit by hand, one after another,
slowly, by whoever wished to learn.

Edrin lived many more years, and he taught a great many children, and the line of
gold climbed the hill every evening just as it always had. He had been right all
along, in his quiet way: some things are worth doing slowly, not because slow is
better than fast, but because the going is the part you remember.
